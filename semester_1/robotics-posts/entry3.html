The first thing I did this week was watch YouTube videos of other people's Arduino RC projects, as many online resources are available. After looking at multiple, I came across the same type of communication used across most of them. This is the nRF24L01, for which I have linked a website from which I got most of my learning. The nRF24L01 is a low-power wireless transceiver module that operates at 2.4GHz and is popular for Arduino projects due to its efficiency and ease of use. It supports data rates of up to 2 Mbps and offers a range of about 100 meters in open spaces, which will be plenty for the plane as we will mostly fly it in open fields such as an oval. With features like multiple communication channels, auto-acknowledgement of packets, and a simple SPI interface (Serial Peripheral Interface) for communication, which is straightforward to implement with microcontrollers like Arduino, the nRF24L01 is a versatile solution for my project.

There are multiple variations of the nRF24L01 series, all with different capabilities, the most common being the nRF24L01, nRF24L01+, and nRF24L01P. The standard nRF24L01 offers a range of about 100 meters and operates at a data rate of up to 2mps. The nRF24L01+ improves on this with a longer range of up to 1000 meters and includes features like a low-dropout regulator and dynamic payload length. Finally, the nRF24L01P has all the enhancements of the plus version but offers even better power-saving features and reliability in tougher communication environments. Each version is designed to cater to different needs in wireless communication applications.

After considering all my options, I searched on Amazon for the nRF24L01 to get it as soon as possible. Initially, I was only looking for the nRF24L01+, but I came across a great bundle that included two nRF24L01 modules and two nRF24L01+ modules, all at a much lower price than other alternatives. Although the base nRF24L01 would suffice for my immediate needs, I realized that investing a little more for the enhanced versions would benefit my future projects. Ultimately, I concluded that having better quality modules would make me much happier and provide more versatility for upcoming applications.
