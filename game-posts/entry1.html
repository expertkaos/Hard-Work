Game Development

Entry 1 

date, 2025

This week marked the first week of a new semester. With that, I had the option to either continue my project from the previous semester or start a new one. Looking back on my old project, I found that I was upset with the game concept. Ultimately, it was merely a poor imitation of the game rounds. I could fix this problem and take the game into a completely new direction. Still, at this point, the game wasn't giving me enough motivation to continue with it, so I have decided it would be a better idea to create a new game with a completely new concept and with this, I can use the knowledge that I learnt while making my previous game to make this one even better. That led me to where I am now: what will be my new game?

I began brainstorming game ideas and started thinking about games that I like and enjoy. Looking at games that I enjoy provides a foundation for my thoughts, while also allowing me to create a game that I am passionate about. This knowledge of the game genre comes from my enjoyment of it.

I had this idea for a game inspired by "Bob the Robber," but with a fun twist: instead of stealing stuff, you’re actually returning it. I thought it would be hilarious and would give the character, a former criminal, a chance at redemption. But then I hit a snag with the gameplay. Unlike the usual sneaky grabbing of valuables, returning items just didn't seem as exciting. I played around with the idea of using outlines to show where to put things, but that felt way too limiting and might take away from the fun. So, after thinking it over, I decided to ditch the idea and move on to something new. So I went back to the drawing board.

So I went back to the drawing board.

To get inspiration, I looked through my Steam library and checked out games I've been sinking the most hours into lately. Two stood out immediately: DayZ and Sons of the Forest. Something cool about survival games that has always intrigued me is that all survival games at their base are the same, managing stats, upgrading gear, crafting tools and armour, staying alive; they all do it in their own ways, which makes each one unique and fun in its way.

In Dayz, the story is more in the background. It's a zombie apocalypse with a heavy PvP focus. You're constantly on edge, scavenging and trying to survive while avoiding (or confronting) other players, and when you do, your heart will race faster than you could imagine. It's chaotic, unpredictable and honestly, stressful in a good way. I focus heavily on trying to survive.

While Sons of the Forest, on the other hand, leans much more into the narrative. Sure, you still have to scavenge and survive like in Dayz, but here’s also a stronger sense of mystery and story progression that keeps you hooked differently.

And then there's another game I've been playing recently: Subnautica. It's also a survival game, but it stands out for its focus on exploration, crafting and base building within a completely different environment compared to other survival games out there, due to it being purely set underwater. There’s a bit of story in there, too, but the way it balances survival and creativity makes it satisfying to play.

Out of all three, Subnautica is one I enjoy most, especially when I think about it from a game development perspective. The building aspect, in particular, really appeals to me.

After putting my understanding on survival games and what I like about them, I've settled on the idea of creating a survival game that focuses heavily on crafting and scavenging, which appeals to me about the building aspect. To make my game unique, I envision a time traveller who can only go back in time and has found themselves stranded on a prehistoric Earth. In this world with no agriculture, players will need to craft tools and survive, using a survival guide device that you had with you when you fell back in time, but also your time machine that you can use to get out of tricky situations, solve problems that may be scattered around the map.

The rewind time mechanic could be cool, but because it will take up so much time, I want to focus on the game basics of a survival game. Then once I have that, I can move on to creating a story, working on time travel mechanics.

What made me curious about my game idea, and how can I explore that curiosity further?

What made me curious about my game idea this week was the concept of time travel and how it could change the mechanics of a survival game. I started to think about how I could implement the time machine mechanic to create interesting puzzles throughout the game. Additionally, I considered how it could be used as a tool when fighting enemies.

When thinking about enemies, I imagined them as cavemen, dinosaurs, or animals that lived alongside primitive humans. With this in mind, I can explore multiple ways to utilise the time machine and see what works best, including whether I need to implement a cooldown period for its use.

How am I using what I learned from last semester to improve this semester?

This semester, Im taking what I learned last semester and using it to step up my game. I’m excited about the idea of creating a 3D game, which is a change from my previous work, but through this experience, I have picked up some skills from making other games. I've learnt from last semester that it is important to be passionate about my game and have a good understanding of what I want to create. This is why I fully dedicated myself this week to form a clear idea of what I want to do before even touching the game engine.
