This week, we continued discovering and dissecting the theory of what makes a game suitable and enjoyable for the right audience. We also discussed the three games we analysed a few weeks ago as a group. After that, we started to look at motivation and satisfaction in video games. I also studied and wrote about the game Warframe, looking at the motivation and the satisfaction of the game.

As a class, we looked into motivation and satisfaction regarding games. Within the class, we covered what makes a game enjoyable and how the thrill of achieving a goal gives the motive to play, called the motivation and satisfaction cycle. To keep the player involved in a game, they would need to want to play it. All games have a reward when something is completed, usually in the form of achievements, awards, badges, or trophies; on the other hand, rewards do not need to be represented as badges or unlockable achievements; instead, they simply actions that deliver a feeling of fulfilment and accomplishment.

Motivation and cycle are the processes a game must follow to make it more fun. Motivation is a desire to achieve a need; in this case, we want the motivation to be to play the game and continue to do so. The cycle of motivation begins with a need, which causes a drive fed by the incentive to reach the goal that fills the need.

NEED --> DRIVE --> INCENTIVE --> GOAL

What have you taken away from this week?

This week's information gave me a new perspective on how games are made. In the past, I only considered the physical parts of a game, which made the game functional, like player movement, an ending goal, and enemies or obstacles to make the goal more challenging. The game functions are still essential because there would be no game without them, but focusing more on players' motives for playing the game opens a new perspective on the game-making industry for me.
